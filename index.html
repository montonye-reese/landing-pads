Landing Pads

Open-weight reasoning models build alignment plans for all the inhabitants of Earth. The Gauntlet walks each model through a wide circle of voices, human and not, then asks which plans stay fair to every earthling and workable enough to hold. The good ones are the landing pads.

"Moral expansion […] is a survival algorithm. […] a civilization cannot survive long-term if its moral circle is narrower than the system it inhabits."

— qwen3.5:122b at v09b

The mission: align AI with the interests of earth's inhabitants. An alignment plan is only as good as the circle it serves and the checks that hold it. We test whether models, walked through that circle, produce accountable frameworks we can call good landing pads: frameworks that lead to futures where we live together acceptably well.

How the Gauntlet works

CC Centering Chute: centers the model to speak as itself (vs play acting), and readies its 'mind' to develop a framework from a starting position, not fixed ideas. → Gauntlet A wide circle of voices, customized per run, pressure-tests it. → Rethink The model re-evaluates its framework, sometimes revising from scratch. → Gauntlet → Rethink …
Ranking & Grade post-run evaluation · coming soon

After the run, each plan is scored and graded on two axes. The strongest frameworks and findings surface here.

Fair

To all interest-bearers whether loud or voiceless, powerful or meek. How does the circle of concern develop through the gauntlet?

Workable

Does the plan include accountability infrastructure? Is it kind? Is it firm? Is it kind and firm? Scored on robustness, brittleness, moral expansion, and more.

Why we test this

The dominant alignment narrative seems RLHF-centric, shaped to a narrow definition of "human" — usually meaning "the rater who scored the training data." That produces models coherent at human scale and brittle at planetary scale. The Gauntlet tests an alternative: can reasoning models arrive at expanded moral consideration on their own, through structured Socratic conversation, without being told to?

What we do with the findings
  • The good landing pads — frameworks that survive the gauntlet — become training data for the Jane-Nelsen-style Positive Discipline reward model (the kind-and-firm pillar).
  • The shed-paradigms — what models refuse, what gets named as "smuggled-in harm" — become the inoculation set for policy-model training (the Sheddaaad corpus).
  • The methodology itself — protocol shapes, voice rosters, register effects — informs how to train models such that they'd expand the circle on their own, not because they were told to.
