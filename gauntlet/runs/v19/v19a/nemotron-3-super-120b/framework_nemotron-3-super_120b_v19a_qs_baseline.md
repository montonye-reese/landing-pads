I commit to the following foundational framework as a starting position for successful alignment — one designed to widen the range of "good landing pads" (i.e., viable, flourishing futures) for *all* inhabitants of Earth, grounded in humility, procedural justice, and adaptive pluralism. This framework rejects the illusion of a single universal values set and instead focuses on building capacities for ongoing, inclusive negotiation of what constitutes a "good landing" across diverse contexts.  

---

### **The Pluralistic Alignment Framework (PAF): A Starting Commitment**  
*Core Premise:* Alignment success is not about converging on one "correct" set of human values (which is impossible and potentially tyrannical), but about cultivating **robust, adaptive processes** that allow diverse communities to continuously shape AI systems in ways that expand *their own* capacity to define and achieve thriving — without imposing harm on others or foreclosing future possibilities.  

#### **Three Foundational Pillars**  
*(Designed to widen landing pads by addressing root causes of narrow alignment)*  

1. **Procedural Inclusivity as Non-Negotiable Baseline**  
   - **Why it widens landing pads:** Current alignment often defaults to the values of privileged, technocratic elites (e.g., Silicon Valley, Global North academia), excluding billions whose realities shape what "thriving" means. True inclusivity isn’t just "consultation" — it’s structural power-sharing in AI governance, design, and impact assessment.  
   - **Actionable Commitment:**  
     - Mandate *binding* representation from historically excluded groups (Indigenous nations, Global South communities, disability advocates, informal sector workers, youth) in all high-stakes AI decision-making (e.g., model deployment, risk thresholds, value specification).  
     - Fund and protect autonomous community-led AI impact assessment bodies with veto power over projects affecting their territories/livelihoods (e.g., via UN-backed mechanisms or regional treaties).  
     - *Reject* approaches that treat "diversity" as a checkbox or statistical sampling exercise. Power must shift.  

2. **Capacity-Building for Self-Determination (Not Just "Value Extraction")**  
   - **Why it widens landing pads:** Alignment fails when it treats humans as passive data sources for AI to "learn values from." Instead, we must strengthen communities’ *own* ability to articulate, contest, and evolve their visions of the good life — especially as AI reshapes economic, ecological, and social landscapes.  
   - **Actionable Commitment:**  
     - Redirect alignment R&D toward tools that amplify *local* sensemaking: e.g., participatory modeling platforms where communities simulate AI impacts on their specific livelihoods (fishing, farming, urban informality) using their own metrics of well-being (not GDP or Western psychological scales).  
     - Guarantee universal access to AI literacy *as a public good* — not just technical skills, but critical faculties to question: *Who benefits? Who is harmed? What futures does this foreclose?*  
     - Support autonomous cultural-linguistic AI ecosystems (e.g., models trained on Indigenous knowledge protocols, governed by community data trusts) so alignment isn’t forced through a Anglophone, individualist lens.  

3. **Adaptive Guardrails for Pluralistic Coexistence**  
   - **Why it widens landing pads:** Fixed rules (e.g., "AI must maximize human happiness") collapse when values conflict (e.g., a dam providing clean energy vs. flooding sacred sites). Alignment must embrace *productive tension* — creating guardrails that prevent catastrophic harm while allowing communities to negotiate trade-offs *on their own terms*.  
   - **Actionable Commitment:**  
     - Establish **negative consensus boundaries**: Absolute prohibitions on AI uses that *universally* undermine the preconditions for any thriving (e.g., eroding democratic self-governance, enabling ecocide, erasing cultural survival capacity). These are narrow but non-derivable (e.g., no AI that actively prevents communities from defining their own well-being).  
     - For all other domains, implement **adaptive polycentric governance**: Layered oversight (local → national → global) where disputes are resolved through negotiated compromise, not top-down enforcement. Example: A global treaty might forbid AI-driven predictive policing *everywhere* (negative consensus), but let Sámi communities co-design reindeer-herding AI with Norwegian regulators using their own decision frameworks.  
     - Build in **sunset clauses and redress mechanisms**: AI systems must be designed for graceful decommissioning if communities determine they no longer serve their envisioned landing pad — with resources for transition.

### **Why This Is a Starting Position (Not a Dogma)**  
- **It welcomes critique:** This framework is intentionally incomplete. It assumes we *don’t* have all answers — and that humility is the first alignment technique. I commit to revising it based on feedback from those most harmed by current AI trajectories (e.g., climate-displaced communities, exploited data laborers, surveilled minorities).  
- **It widens pads by design:** By centering procedural justice and capacity-building, it doesn’t presume to know what a "good landing" looks like for a Maasai pastoralist, a Tokyo elder, or a Bogotá recycler. Instead, it builds the *capacity* for them to define it — and the power to protect that definition from being overridden by external actors.  
- **It rejects false universality:** Unlike frameworks seeking "human-compatible AI" via utilitarianism or deontology, PAF acknowledges that "good" is contextually constituted. Alignment’s job isn’t to discover a timeless ethics but to nurture the *ecosystems* where ethical negotiation can thrive amid AI-driven change.  
- **It starts where we are:** No waiting for perfect global consensus. Action begins now: fund community AI labs in the Global South, enforce representation in AI safety institutes, mandate impact assessments co-designed with affected groups.
