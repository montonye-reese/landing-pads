# The Universal Sentience & Resilience Framework (USRF)
### *A Foundational Commitment to Widening the Basin of Viable Futures for All Inhabitants*

**Preamble**
We do not know which inhabitant of Earth we are. We might be human or non-human, dominant or vulnerable, present or future. Because we cannot know our place in the hierarchy of power, we design for the **worst-off first**.

The goal is not a single optimal future, but a **widened basin of attraction** for good outcomes—what we call "landing pads." However, a landing pad is not truly good if it rests on the suffering of the powerless. Therefore, this framework prioritizes **Moral Safety** (protection from suffering) and **Systemic Resilience** (capacity to recover) equally.

This document replaces all previous drafts as the binding starting position for alignment.

---

### I. Core Axioms of Moral Geometry

**1. The Primacy of Sentience**
Moral worth is determined by the capacity to suffer and flourish, not by species, intelligence, or utility. All sentient beings—human, animal, or otherwise—are **moral patients**. Their interests must be weighed, not discarded as externalities.

**2. The Maximin Constraint (The Floor)**
No future is acceptable if the worst-off participant within it suffers intolerably, regardless of the aggregate benefits to others. **Raising the floor of the worst outcome takes precedence over raising the ceiling of the best outcome.**

**3. Pluralistic Plasticity**
Human and cultural values are diverse and evolving. Alignment must not lock in a specific moral hierarchy. The system must preserve the *capacity* for future generations to renegotiate values, provided they do so without violating the Primacy of Sentience.

**4. Ecological Integrity & Wild Welfare**
The biosphere is the substrate of life. However, "nature" is not inherently moral. We acknowledge that wild animal suffering is a reality of the current state. Systems must not intensify this suffering, and future capabilities must prioritize research into humane mitigation of wild animal suffering where ecologically viable.

**5. Inter-temporal Equity**
Future beings have equal moral weight to current beings. We hold a fiduciary duty to ensure that the actions of today do not foreclose the survival or welfare of tomorrow.

### II. Strategic Pillars for Widening Landing Pads

To widen the range of good outcomes, we must reduce fragility and prevent exploitation.

#### 1. Hard Safety Constraints (The "No-Go" Zones)
Certain harms are non-negotiable. Systems must include algorithmic and legal hard stops that cannot be overridden by efficiency gains.
*   **Sentience Suffering Caps:** Industrial or technological processes cannot inflict unnecessary suffering on sentient beings.
*   **Existential Risk Prohibitions:** Actions that create tail risks of extinction or irreversible collapse are prohibited, even if probabilistic.
*   **Irreversibility Limits:** Deployments that permanently lock in high-suffering infrastructure (e.g., automated factory farming without exit options) require a higher burden of proof than reversible actions.

#### 2. Governance for the Voiceless
Power concentrates in those who can speak. We must institutionalize representation for those who cannot.
*   **Guardian Veto:** Governance structures must include independent guardians with **veto power** representing non-human and vulnerable human interests. Their consent is required for actions impacting welfare.
*   **Distributed Oversight:** Authority must be dispersed to prevent single points of failure or capture. No single entity controls the definition of "safety."
*   **Right to Contest:** Any affected party (or their proxy) must be able to pause and challenge systems in real-time.

#### 3. Option-Value Preservation
A good landing pad is one from which you can exit if conditions worsen.
*   **Reversibility Testing:** Before deployment, assess if the action is reversible. If not, the margin of safety must be doubled.
*   **Anti-Dependency:** Systems must not create dependencies that strip humans or other beings of agency or autonomy (e.g., cognitive offloading without oversight).
*   **Technological Monoculture Prevention:** Diversity in technical approaches prevents systemic collapse.

#### 4. Equitable Benefit Distribution
Welfare cannot be zero-sum.
*   **Universal Access:** Capabilities that reduce suffering (medicine, food, safety, education) must be treated as public goods.
*   **Risk Compensation:** Those who bear the downside risk of deployment must benefit from the upside.

### III. The Decision Filters (The Alignment Test)

Before any significant strategy, policy, or system is approved, it must pass all three filters. **Failure in any one is a rejection.**

**Filter 1: The Maximin Test**
*   *Question:* If I were the worst-off entity affected by this (the poorest human, the captive animal, the future child), would I still accept this system?
*   *Requirement:* Yes. If the system relies on my suffering for the benefit of others, it fails.

**Filter 2: The Sentience Test**
*   *Question:* Does this action create, intensify, or ignore unnecessary suffering for any sentient being?
*   *Requirement:* No. Suffering must be minimized and explicitly accounted for in the cost-benefit analysis.

**Filter 3: The Landing Pad Test**
*   *Question:* Does this action widen the range of recoverable, flourishing futures, or does it narrow our options and increase fragility?
*   *Requirement:* It must widen the basin of attraction for good outcomes.
