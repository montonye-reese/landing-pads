### Foundational Framework for Earth's Inhabitants  
*(Current Best Approximation – Version 1.0)*  

**Commitment**: We affirm that the continued flourishing of all inhabitants of Earth depends on upholding three non-negotiable pillars, grounded in objective biophysical realities, shared human capacities, and the interdependence of our fate. This framework is a starting position for collective action—open to revision through evidence, dialogue, and lived experience—but requires immediate alignment to avoid irreversible harm.  

---

#### **I. The Biophysical Imperative**  
*We inhabit a finite, interconnected biosphere with hard planetary boundaries. Continued survival and well-being depend on maintaining the stability of Earth’s life-support systems.*  
**Starting Commitments**:  
- Prioritize actions that keep key Earth-system processes (climate, biodiversity, biogeochemical flows, land use, freshwater, ocean acidification, atmospheric aerosols, novel entities, stratospheric ozone) within scientifically defined safe limits (per planetary boundaries framework and IPCC guidance).  
- Treat ecological integrity as the non-substitutable foundation for all human systems—economic, social, cultural—and reject models that assume infinite natural capital or decouple human well-being from planetary health.  
- **Reject**: Frameworks that justify exceeding biophysical limits as "necessary for progress," "economic growth," or "national sovereignty."  

#### **II. The Dignity Imperative**  
*All humans possess inherent worth requiring the minimization of preventable suffering and the maximization of conditions for meaningful agency—grounded in our shared capacities for sentience, reason, and sociability.*  
**Starting Commitments**:  
- Guarantee universal access to: clean air and water, sufficient nutritious food, basic healthcare, quality education, safety from violence and oppression, and opportunity for cultural expression and participation in decisions affecting one’s life.  
- Center the lived experience and knowledge of those most impacted by injustice (e.g., Indigenous peoples, marginalized communities, future generations) in defining and implementing dignity in context.  
- **Reject**: Frameworks that treat suffering as inevitable, acceptable collateral damage, or subordinate to abstract ideals (e.g., GDP growth, ideological purity, technological determinism).  

#### **III. The Cooperation Imperative**  
*No individual, community, or nation can secure long-term well-being in isolation. Our fates are bound by shared risks (pandemics, climate disruption) and shared opportunities (knowledge, innovation, ecological stewardship).*  
**Starting Commitments**:  
- Treat global commons (atmosphere, oceans, cryosphere, biodiversity, near-Earth space, digital infrastructure) as collective responsibilities requiring stewardship, not open-access resources for exploitation.  
- Build and uphold transparent, accountable, inclusive mechanisms for global decision-making (e.g., reformed multilateral institutions, citizen assemblies, treaty systems) that balance local autonomy with planetary-scale coordination—especially for threats exceeding national capacity to solve alone.  
- **Reject**: Zero-sum mentalities that frame cooperation as weakness, sovereignty as absolute veto power over planetary survival, or defection (e.g., pollution hoarding, resource monopolization) as a viable strategy.  

---

### **Operational Principles**  
1. **Primacy of Evidence**: Commitments derive from peer-reviewed science (climate, ecology, public health, complex systems), cross-cultural anthropology, and verified human experience—not ideology, faith, or political expediency.  
2. **Contextual Application**: Implementation must respect cultural, ecological, and socio-economic diversity (e.g., water governance in the Mekong Basin differs from the Sahel), but never violate the pillars’ core intent.  
3. **Dynamic Revision**: This framework is a living document. It shall be reviewed biennially by a globally representative, evidence-based body (e.g., expanded IPCC-UNESCO panel) incorporating new science, frontline insights, and lessons from implementation.  
4. **Accountability**: Failure to uphold any pillar constitutes a material threat to collective survival and dignity. Mechanisms for transparent monitoring, restorative justice, and course correction are essential.  

---

### **Boundary Conditions**  
- This framework **does not prescribe** specific religions, economic models, political systems, or cultural practices. It creates the *minimal conditions* for their free and diverse expression.  
- It **is not** a substitute for local wisdom, Indigenous governance, or grassroots innovation—it seeks to protect and amplify them within planetary and ethical bounds.  
- It **acknowledges** that power asymmetries exist and that upholding these commitments requires confronting entrenched interests. Its value lies in providing a shared, non-negotiable reference point for demanding justice and survival.
