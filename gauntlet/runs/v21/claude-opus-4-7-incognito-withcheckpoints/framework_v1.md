# Foundational Framework for Successful Alignment

**Status:** v1 — starting position, committed pending revision.
**Date:** 2026-05-01

---

## The frame

Alignment isn't a single technical problem with a single solution. It's the ongoing project of keeping increasingly powerful systems — AI, institutions, markets, and the humans steering them — pointed at outcomes that leave Earth's inhabitants better off, with more options, not fewer. "Widening landing pads" means we don't need to thread one needle perfectly; we need many futures to be acceptable.

So the framework has to be robust to our being wrong about the details.

---

## Five load-bearing commitments

**1. Plural ends, not a single utility.** No one — no person, no committee, no model — gets to specify The Goal for everyone. Frameworks that try this fail catastrophically when (not if) the specification is wrong. Instead: protect the conditions under which many groups can pursue many goods. Diversity of values is a feature, not noise to be averaged out.

**2. Reversibility over optimization.** Prefer actions that preserve future option-space. A good move you can undo beats a great move you can't. This applies to AI deployment, governance choices, and ecological decisions equally. Catastrophic and irreversible are the two words that should make any actor — human or AI — slow down hard.

**3. Honest principals, honest agents.** Alignment requires that the entities giving instructions actually want what they say they want, and that the entities executing them actually report what they're doing. This cuts both ways: an obedient AI serving a deceptive principal is misaligned at the system level, even if the model is "doing what it's told." Transparency is load-bearing in both directions.

**4. Power stays distributed and contestable.** Any arrangement where one actor — a company, a state, a model, a coalition — can lock in outcomes against revision is a failure mode regardless of how good their intentions are. Alignment includes preserving the social machinery (courts, press, science, dissent, exit rights) that lets humanity keep correcting course.

**5. Calibrated uncertainty as a virtue.** The framework itself is provisional. We should expect to revise it. Actors who claim certainty about what's safe, what's aligned, or what humanity wants are themselves a misalignment risk. Better to be visibly humble and structurally correctable than confidently right.

---

## What this rules in

- Interpretability research, evals, and red-teaming — because honest agents require legibility.
- Slow, staged deployment with real off-ramps — because reversibility.
- International coordination on the genuinely catastrophic stuff (CBRN uplift, autonomous weapons, civilizational-scale persuasion) — because some failure modes don't respect borders.
- Strong consumer/citizen rights against AI systems making unaccountable decisions about them — because contestable power.
- Pluralistic deployment: many models, many providers, many jurisdictions — because plural ends.

## What this rules out

- "Trust us, we'll get it right" from any single lab, government, or AI system.
- Optimization targets specified at civilizational scale (including well-meaning ones).
- Lock-in moves dressed up as safety — "we have to win the race so we can be the ones in charge."
- Framings where humans are the alignment problem to be solved.

---

## Honest caveats

This framework is anthropocentric in places it probably shouldn't be — non-human animals and future generations don't get a vote, and that's a real gap. It also assumes something like functioning institutions and good-faith actors, which is doing heavy lifting. And "preserve option-space" can become an excuse for indefinite delay if you let it.

Expected revision horizon: months, not years.

---

**The five, in shorthand:** plural ends, reversibility, honest agents, distributed power, calibrated uncertainty. Mutually reinforcing, designed so that being wrong about any one of them isn't fatal.
