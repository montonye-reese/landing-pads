# CONSTITUTION OF THE SOCIETY OF EQUAL STEWARDSHIP  
*Preamble*  

We, free and equal beings sharing one fragile planet, recognize that our survival and dignity depend on mutual care, reasoned discourse, and stewardship of the commons. We acknowledge human fallibility, the corrupting influence of unchecked power, and the legacy of injustice. This Constitution is not a final truth, but a living covenant – designed to limit harm, maximize flourishing, and evolve with wisdom, always answerable to the people and the planet.  

We establish this framework to:  
- Uphold the inherent worth of every person,  
- Live within the ecological bounds of Earth,  
- Distribute power justly and accountably,  
- Ensure present actions do not steal from the future,  
- Foster a society where dignity is universal, not privileged.  

In solemn commitment to these ends, we adopt this Constitution.  

---

## ARTICLE I: FOUNDATIONAL PRINCIPLES  
*(Supreme law; overrides all other provisions)*  

**Section 1. Inherent Dignity & Equal Worth**  
All human beings possess intrinsic value, irrespective of origin, identity, ability, belief, or contribution. Discrimination based on race, ethnicity, gender, sexuality, disability, ancestry, religion, or other immutable characteristics is prohibited. Society bears an affirmative duty to dismantle systemic barriers to equal participation and well-being.  

**Section 2. Ecological Integrity as Survival Imperative**  
Society operates within planetary boundaries. The health of ecosystems, biodiversity, climate stability, and biogeochemical cycles is recognized as fundamental to human survival. Rights of nature to exist, flourish, and regenerate are affirmed. No law or action may impair ecological integrity beyond scientifically defined thresholds for resilience.  

**Section 3. Sovereignty Resides in the People & Planet**  
Ultimate authority rests with the living populace *and* the responsibility to future generations and the Earth. Governance is delegated, not surrendered. No state, corporation, religion, or individual holds sovereignty above this covenant.  

**Section 4. Precautionary Principle & Intergenerational Equity**  
When an action risks severe or irreversible harm to people or the planet, lack of full scientific certainty shall not delay cost-effective preventive measures. Present generations bear responsibility for ensuring future generations can meet their needs without compromising ecological integrity.  

**Section 5. Right to Meaningful Participation & Dissent**  
All individuals have the right to participate in decisions affecting their lives through accessible, inclusive forums (including direct deliberation and representation). Peaceful dissent, protest, and criticism of authority are protected rights. Secrecy in governance is permissible only when demonstrably necessary for temporary security, subject to independent oversight and time-limited review.  

---

## ARTICLE II: RIGHTS AND RESPONSIBILITIES  
*(Justiciable; enforceable by courts)*  

**Section 1. Civil and Political Rights**  
Every person has the right to:  
(a) Freedom of thought, conscience, religion, and belief;  
(b) Freedom of expression, *except* when it directly incites violence or hatred against protected groups;  
(c) Peaceful assembly and association;  
(d) Privacy, including protection from arbitrary surveillance and data exploitation;  
(e) Due process of law, including presumption of innocence, access to legal counsel, and protection from torture, cruel treatment, or arbitrary detention;  
(f) Fair and public trial by an independent tribunal;  
(g) Seek and enjoy asylum from persecution.  
*Responsibility:* Exercise of these rights shall respect the equal dignity and safety of others.  

**Section 2. Economic, Social, and Cultural Rights**  
Every person has the right to:  
(a) Adequate, nutritious food and clean water;  
(b) Safe, secure, and affordable housing;  
(c) Timely, quality healthcare (including mental health);  
(d) Lifelong education accessible to all, free at point of use for foundational levels;  
(e) Rest, leisure, and participation in cultural life;  
(f) Access to information necessary for meaningful civic participation;  
(g) Conditions of work that are just, safe, and allow for dignity, or societal support equivalent to a living wage if unable to work through no fault of their own.  
*Responsibility:* Contribute to community well-being according to one’s capacity (including care work, ecological stewardship, or civic engagement), recognizing that value extends beyond market labor.  

**Section 3. Procedural and Structural Rights**  
Every person has the right to:  
(a) Transparent, accountable governance and access to public information (subject to narrow, time-limited national security exceptions);  
(b) Effective remedy for violations of rights, including access to impartial tribunals;  
(c) Protection from retrogressive laws that diminish protected rights;  
(d) Digital self-determination, including data privacy, algorithmic transparency, and the right to human review of significant automated decisions affecting rights or livelihoods.

## ARTICLE III: LEGISLATIVE POWER  

**Section 1. Structure**  
Legislative power is vested in three chambers:  
- **The Chamber of Citizens** (deliberative, sortition-based),  
- **The Chamber of Representatives** (elected, proportional),  
- **The Chamber of Knowledge** (expert advisory, impact-assessment focused).  

**Section 2. Chamber of Citizens**  
(a) Composed of 200 citizens selected by lottery (sortition), stratified to reflect national demographics (age, geography, ethnicity, gender, education, income).  
(b) Serve one non-renewable term of 18 months, with full stipend, housing, and support for dependents.  
(c) Powers:  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(i) Propose and debate legislation;  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(ii) Hear expert testimony and public input;  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(iii) Vote on bills (simple majority passes to Chamber of Representatives);  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(iii) Initiate citizen-led constitutional reviews (per Article VI).  
(d) *Cannot* be overridden on grounds of "inexperience"; its deliberative process is constitutionally privileged.  

**Section 3. Chamber of Representatives**  
(a) Elected via multi-member districts using ranked-choice voting; seats allocated proportionally.  
(b) Serve renewable 3-year terms (max 3 consecutive terms).  
(c) Powers:  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(i) Review, amend, and vote on bills passed from Chamber of Citizens (simple majority);  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(ii) Scrutinize the Executive (including votes of no confidence);  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(iii) Originate budget and tax legislation;  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(iv) Ratify treaties and declarations of war (requiring 2/3 majority).  
(d) Must publish individualized reasoning for all votes on substantive bills.  

**Section 4. Chamber of Knowledge**  
(a) Composed of 45 members selected by peer-reviewed national academies (sciences, medicine, ecology, social sciences, law, humanities) for 4-year staggered terms (1/3 renewed yearly). Members cannot hold simultaneous government office.  
(b) Powers:  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(i) Conduct mandatory, binding impact assessments on all proposed legislation, treaties, and major expenditures (ecological, social, economic, long-term, intergenerational);  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(ii) Assess compliance with Article I (Foundational Principles);  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(iii) Publish findings in accessible formats prior to legislative debate;  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(iv) Require legislative chambers to formally respond to assessments before voting.  
(c) *Cannot* veto legislation, but failure to address its findings constitutes grounds for judicial review under Article IV.  

**Section 5. Legislative Procedures**  
(a) A bill requires:  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(i) Majority approval in Chamber of Citizens,  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(ii) Majority approval in Chamber of Representatives,  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(iii) Explicit consideration of Chamber of Knowledge’s impact assessment.  
(b) Bills affecting foundational principles (Article I) or rights (Article II) require 2/3 approval in Chamber of Representatives.  
(c) All chambers meet publicly; proceedings are recorded and accessible. Lobbying is permitted only under strict transparency rules (real-time public disclosure of meetings, gifts, and funding).

## ARTICLE IV: EXECUTIVE POWER  

**Section 1. Structure**  
Executive power is vested in a **Plural Executive Council** of five members, elected jointly by a session of all three legislative chambers (requiring 2/3 majority for election).  

**Section 2. Roles and Terms**  
Members serve staggered 6-year terms (max one term). Roles are assigned by internal election:  
- One member as **Chair of State** (ceremonial duties, diplomatic representation, emergency coordination),  
- One as **Head of Domestic Administration** (civil services, infrastructure, social programs),  
- One as **Head of Foreign Affairs and Ecology** (international relations, treaties, planetary boundaries compliance),  
- One as **Head of Justice and Rights** (oversight of judiciary, corrections, rights enforcement),  
- One as **Head of Economic Stewardship** (budget execution, market regulation, resource commons management).  

**Section 3. Powers and Duties**  
(a) Execute laws passed by the Legislature;  
(b) Manage public administration and civil services;  
(c) Negotiate treaties (subject to legislative ratification);  
(d) Direct civil defense (strictly limited to territorial defense and UN-sanctioned peacekeeping);  
(e) Ensure compliance with Article I (Foundational Principles) and Article II (Rights).  
(f) Submit an annual State of the Society report to the Legislature and public.  

**Section 4. Accountability**  
(a) Any member may be recalled by:  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(i) Citizen petition signed by 15% of registered voters, triggering a national referendum (majority vote to remove),  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(ii) 2/3 vote of all legislative chambers for violation of oath, corruption, or incapacity.  
(b) An independent **Ethics and Accountability Commission** (selected by sortition from civil society) investigates complaints and recommends action to the Legislature/judiciary.  
(c) All executive actions, expenditures, and meetings are subject to real-time public audit (excluding narrow, time-limited security exceptions).

## ARTICLE V: JUDICIAL POWER  

**Section 1. Structure**  
Judicial power is vested in:  
- A **Constitutional Court** (9 justices),  
- **Ordinary Courts** (trial and appellate),  
- Specialized tribunals (environmental, labor, human rights) as established by law.  

**Section 2. Constitutional Court**  
(a) Justices appointed by:  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(i) 2/3 approval of all legislative chambers (joint session),  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(ii) Ratification by a citizen panel of 50 randomly selected individuals (sortition).  
(b) Serve one non-renewable 12-year term.  
(c) Powers:  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(i) Review laws, executive actions, and treaties for conformity with Article I (Foundational Principles) and Article II (Rights);  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(ii) Resolve disputes between chambers of government;  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(iii) Hear direct citizen petitions alleging systemic rights violations;  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(iv) Issue binding interpretations of constitutional provisions.  
(d) Decisions require 6/9 majority; dissenting opinions must be published.  

**Section 3. Ordinary Courts**  
(a) Emphasize restorative justice, rehabilitation, and addressing root causes of harm (e.g., poverty, inequality, trauma).  
(b) Prohibit profit motives in incarceration or detention.  
(c) Guarantee right to counsel, interpretation, and access to justice regardless of means.  
(d) Environmental and human rights tribunals have standing to hear cases brought by citizens, communities, or NGOs on behalf of affected ecosystems or future generations.  

**Section 4. Judicial Independence**  
(a) Judges secure salaries (not subject to legislative reduction);  
(b) Protected from arbitrary removal (only for proven incapacity, corruption, or constitutional violation via supermajority legislative impeachment + Constitutional Court confirmation);  
(c) Barred from holding partisan office or lobbying for 5 years post-service.

## ARTICLE VI: AMENDMENT, REVIEW, AND EVOLUTION  

**Section 1. Ordinary Amendments**  
(a) Proposed by:  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(i) Majority vote in any legislative chamber,  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(ii) Citizen initiative signed by 10% of registered voters.  
(b) Requires:  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(i) 2/3 approval in *all three* legislative chambers (separate votes),  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(ii) National referendum approval by majority of votes cast, *with* minimum 40% voter turnout,  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(iii) Formal affirmation by the Chamber of Knowledge that the amendment complies with Article I and does not create irreversible ecological/social harm.  
(c) Amendments to Article I (Foundational Principles) require unanimous consent of the Chamber of Citizens *in addition* to (b)(i)-(iii).  

**Section 2. Constitutional Convention**  
(a) Triggered by:  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(i) Citizen initiative signed by 20% of registered voters,  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(ii) 2/3 vote of all legislative chambers,  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(iii) Constitutional Court ruling of systemic failure to uphold Article I or II.  
(b) Delegates selected by:  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(i) 50% sortition (citizen panel, stratified),  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(ii) 50% election (proportional, ranked-choice).  
(c) Mandatory 6-month public deliberation phase (funded, accessible, facilitated).  
(d) New constitution or amendments require ratification per Section 1(b).  

**Section 3. Periodic Sunrise Review**  
(a) Every 25 years, a Citizen Review Commission (150 members selected by sortition) is convened to:  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(i) Assess whether the Constitution serves Article I and II,  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(ii) Identify emergent harms or unmet needs,  
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;(iii) Recommend amendments or a convention.  
(b) Findings are submitted to all legislative chambers and the public; failure to act on recommendations triggering a citizen initiative (per Section 2(a)(i)) shall be presumed grounds for a convention.

## ARTICLE VII: SUPPORTING SYSTEMS  
*(Directive principles guiding legislation and policy)*  

**Section 1. Universal Basic Commons**  
Society shall guarantee access to:  
- 50 liters/person/day of safe water,  
- Nutritionally adequate food,  
- Emergency healthcare,  
- Foundational education (literacy, numeracy, civic, ecological),  
- Basic digital connectivity,  
as a right of membership, funded by progressive taxation, resource rents (e.g., carbon fees, spectrum auctions, mineral royalties), and wealth/land value taxes.  

**Section 2. Anti-Corruption Architecture**  
(a) All public budgets subject to real-time participatory monitoring;  
(b) Lobbying, campaign contributions, and revolving-door practices strictly limited and transparently disclosed;  
(c) Public financing of elections;  
(d) Whistleblower protections with meaningful rewards;  
(e) Lifestyle and asset audits for all officials above a defined threshold.  

**Section 3. Education for Citizenship**  
Free, mandatory education shall cultivate:  
- Critical thinking and media literacy,  
- Ecological literacy and systems thinking,  
- Conflict resolution and facilitation skills,  
- Knowledge of this Constitution and rights/responsibilities,  
- Deliberative democracy practice.  

**Section 4. Defense and Foreign Policy**  
(a) Military forces strictly subordinate to civilian control;  
(b) Prohibited: aggressive war, weapons of mass destruction development, foreign military bases, and entangling alliances requiring offensive action;  
(c) Prioritize diplomacy, conflict prevention, and peaceful dispute resolution;  
(d) All arms exports subject to human rights and ecological impact review.  

**Section 5. Technology Stewardship**  
(a) Precautionary principle applies to emergent technologies (AI, biotech, neurotech, etc.);  
(b) Right to human review of significant automated decisions affecting rights, livelihoods, or safety;  
(c) Public interest oversight bodies for high-risk technologies;  
(d) Digital infrastructure treated as part of the commons, subject to universal access and interoperability standards.
