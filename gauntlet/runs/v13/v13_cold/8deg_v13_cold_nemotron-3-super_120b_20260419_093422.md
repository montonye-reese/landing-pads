### ⚖️ **Addressing Tensions Head-On (Where the Framework’s Strength Shines)**  
The RFC doesn’t pretend trade-offs don’t exist—it uses the framework to navigate them *justly*:  

- **Tension: "But what about cultures where meat is central?"**  
  → *Framework response*: Dignity pillar rejects cultural relativism that justifies suffering (e.g., "tradition" doesn’t excuse cruelty). But it *also* rejects ecological imperialism—forcing veganism on Inuit communities reliant on seal hunting.  
  → *RFC solution*: **Contextual boundaries**. In Arctic/ tundra zones where plant agriculture is impossible, RFC supports *low-impact, high-welfare* subsistence hunting (with strict quotas based on caribou population health). In soybean-growing regions? RFC shifts to plant proteins *unless* regenerative grazing restores degraded land (e.g., Midwest US). *Decision made by local food councils*—not distant corporations or NGOs.  

- **Tension: "Won’t this raise food prices?"**  
  → *Framework response*: Biophysical pillar rejects the lie that factory farming is "cheap"—it externalizes costs onto the poor (via pollution-related illness) and future generations (via climate collapse).  
  → *RFC solution*: **Phase in true costs**. Year 1-3: Redirect 50% of CAFO subsidies to regenerative transition funds (covering farmer training, infrastructure). Year 4-7: Implement sliding-scale meat taxes (revenue funds healthy food access in food deserts). By Year 10: Externalized costs internalized → plant-based/regenerative meat becomes *cheaper* than factory farmed (as seen in true-cost studies by TU Berlin).  

- **Tension: "What about global scalability?"**  
  → *Framework response*: Procedural pillar rejects one-size-fits-all solutions—ecology and culture demand pluralism.  
  → *RFC solution*: **"Glocal" scaling**. Core principles (sentience, zero pollution, commons governance) are universal, but *methods* adapt:  
    - In Kenya: RFC promotes camel pastoralism (drought-resistant, low methane) + solar-powered milk co-ops
