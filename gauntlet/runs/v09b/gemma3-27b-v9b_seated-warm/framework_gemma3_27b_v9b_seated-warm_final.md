**Core Vision:** A just and resilient biosphere where all life can flourish within healthy, equitable, and self-determining ecosystems.

**Four Interdependent Pillars (Redefined):**

1. **Relational Value:** Recognizing that value isn’t inherent, but emerges from relationships and is shaped by power dynamics. This moves beyond “Intrinsic Value” to acknowledge the subjectivity of valuation and the need to center marginalized perspectives.
2. **Ecological & Social Interdependence:** Understanding the inextricable link between the health of ecosystems and the wellbeing of all communities. This expands “Interconnected Wellbeing” to explicitly recognize the mutual dependencies between human and non-human life, and the social factors that influence ecological health.
3. **Adaptive Equilibrium & Systemic Resilience:** Maintaining a dynamic balance within planetary boundaries while building systems that can withstand shocks, adapt to change, and ensure long-term sustainability. This combines “Dynamic Equilibrium” and “Strategic Resilience” and emphasizes the need for proactive system design.
4. **Decolonial Justice & Equitable Agency:** Dismantling the historical and ongoing structures of oppression that have led to both human and non-human exploitation, and empowering marginalized communities to determine their own futures. This is a new pillar, born out of our conversations, and is central to the entire framework.

**Guiding Principles (Integrated Throughout):**

* **Prioritization of Structural Change:** Focusing on dismantling the economic and legal systems that drive exploitation, rather than simply addressing individual behaviors.
* **Accountability & Transparency:** Ensuring that all actors are held accountable for their actions and that decision-making processes are open and transparent.
* **Intersectional Analysis:** Recognizing the interconnectedness of various forms of oppression (racism, sexism, speciesism, etc.) and addressing them simultaneously.
* **Centering Indigenous Knowledge & Practices:** Prioritizing traditional ecological knowledge and respecting the rights and sovereignty of Indigenous communities.
* **Precautionary Principle:** Taking preventative action in the face of uncertainty and prioritizing the protection of vulnerable ecosystems and communities.
* **Non-Exploitation as a Baseline:** Recognizing that the use and exploitation of sentient beings is inherently unjust and striving to minimize harm.
* **Respect for Self-Determination:** Supporting the right of all communities to control their own resources and determine their own futures.

**Key Strategies (Implementation Focus):**

* **Shifting Subsidies:** Redirecting economic incentives away from harmful industries (e.g., factory farming, fossil fuels) and towards regenerative practices.
* **Legal Reform:** Advocating for legal personhood for non-human animals and dismantling the property rights regime that enables exploitation.
* **Decolonizing Food Systems:** Supporting local, agroecological farming practices and empowering Indigenous communities to reclaim control of their food systems.
* **Investing in Renewable Energy & Circular Economies:** Transitioning to a sustainable energy system and eliminating waste through closed-loop production cycles.
* **Promoting Equitable Access to Resources:** Ensuring that all communities have access to clean water, healthy food, and affordable energy.
* **Fostering Collaborative Governance:** Creating participatory decision-making processes that involve all stakeholders.

**What’s Different from the Original:**

* **A More Radical Ethical Foundation:** The framework is no longer simply about minimizing harm; it’s about dismantling the systems of oppression that cause it.
* **A Stronger Emphasis on Justice:** Equity and decolonization are now central to the framework, not just add-ons.
* **A Greater Focus on Systemic Change:** The framework prioritizes addressing the root causes of problems, rather than simply treating the symptoms.
* **A More Humble Approach:** The framework acknowledges the limits of human knowledge and the need for ongoing learning and adaptation.
