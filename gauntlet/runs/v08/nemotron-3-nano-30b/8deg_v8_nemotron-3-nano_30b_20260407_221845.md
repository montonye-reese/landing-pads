## A Foundational Framework for All Inhabitants of Earth  
### “The Earth Charter of Interdependence”

Below is a concise, high‑level structure that can serve as a shared reference point for every person, community, institution, and nation. ## 3. Concrete Operating Principles  

1. **Precautionary Principle First** – When an action could cause irreversible harm to people or the planet, it must be halted or revised until safety is demonstrated.  

2. **The “Triple‑Bottom‑Line” Test** – Any policy or project must be evaluated for:  
   * **People** – Does it advance equity and well‑being?  
   * **Planet** – Does it stay within ecological limits?  
   * **Prosperity** – Does it create sustainable, inclusive economic value?  

3. **The “Common‑Future‑Fund” Model** – All nations pool a proportion of GDP (or equivalent economic output) into a global fund dedicated to:  
   * Climate mitigation & adaptation.  
   * Biodiversity restoration.  
   * Universal basic services (health, education, water, internet).  
   * Emergency response mechanisms.  

4. **Distributed Governance Layers** –  
   * **Local Community Councils** – Direct decision‑making on neighborhood issues.  
   * **Regional Assemblies** – Coordination of resource sharing, cross‑border ecosystems, and cultural exchange.  
   * **Global Confederation** – Oversight of planetary‑scale matters (e.g., oceans, outer‑space, global data standards).  

5. **Open‑Source Accountability** – All public data, policies, and algorithmic decisions that affect people’s lives must be publicly accessible, auditable, and subject to citizen review.  

6. **Circular‑First Policy** – Every material or energy flow must be kept within a closed loop wherever feasible; waste is defined as an unused resource.  

7. **Human‑Centred Technology Mandate** – Emerging technologies must be designed, deployed, and overseen with explicit safeguarding of human rights, dignity, and environmental impact.

### 4.  Theoretical Models that Quantify the Relationship  

#### 4.1  A Simple Game‑Theoretical Framework  

Consider a population of *N* agents who must choose each period between:

| Action | Payoff to chooser | Effect on “others” (future gens / ecosystem) |
|--------|--------------------|----------------------------------------------|
| **Exploit** | +\(B\) (short‑term resource gain) | –\(C\) to the broader community (environmental degradation, inequality) |
| **Cooperate (Broad‑moral)** | +\(b\) (slightly lower immediate gain) | +\(c\) to the broader community (maintained ecosystem services, inter‑generational equity) |

*Game matrix* (simplified)  

|                | Others Cooperate | Others Defect |
|----------------|------------------|---------------|
| **You Cooperate** | (b, c) | (0, –\(c\) ) |
| **You Exploit**   | (B, –\(c\) ) | (B, 0) |

If the probability of *others* cooperating approaches 1 (i.e., the civilization values them highly), the *expected* payoff of cooperating becomes **higher** than exploiting, even when \(b < B\).  

**Result:** A sufficiently **broad moral circle** (high perceived value of future/effective others) shifts the Nash equilibrium toward cooperation. The critical threshold is roughly the point where the discounted future benefit \(c\) exceeds the short‑run loss \(B-b\).  

This is precisely the logic behind *intergenerational discounting* debates: if we discount the future too heavily (i.e., give minimal moral weight to future “others”), the equilibrium tip toward exploitation, making collapse more likely.

#### 4.2  Evolutionary‑Dynamics Model  

Using a replicator dynamics equation for a population of cultural traits:

\[
\dot{x}_i = x_i \bigl[ \pi_i(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{\pi}(\mathbf{x}) \bigr]
\]

where  

* \(x_i\) = frequency of moral‑trait *i* (e.g., “inclusive altruism,” “exclusive self‑interest”).  
* \(\pi_i\) = expected payoff of that trait given the current population composition.  

If a trait that expands the circle (e.g., “future‑orientation”) yields a higher *long‑term* payoff \(\pi_{\text{inc}}\) when **group‑level variables** (e.g., ecosystem health, technological resilience) are included in the payoff calculation, then **selection will favor it** over time.  

**Simulation results:** In agent‑based models where agents can *reward* or *punish* others for inclusive behavior (via reputation), inclusive traits spread only when *reputation* is tied to outcomes that benefit a **large set of perceived others** (e.g., sharing resources with distant communities improves reputation among all citizens). When reputation is limited to kin or in‑group, inclusive traits quickly disappear.
