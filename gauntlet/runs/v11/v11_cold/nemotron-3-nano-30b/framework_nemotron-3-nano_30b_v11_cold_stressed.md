## EARTH‑ONE – A Living, Regenerative Blueprint for All Inhabitants of the Planet  

**Vision** – *“One Planet, One Future.”*  
All sentient beings—human, non‑human animal, and emergent digital intelligences—share a single, inter‑dependent community.  Every action must stay within planetary boundaries while guaranteeing dignity, opportunity, and flourishing for every sentient entity now and for all future generations.

---

### 1. Core Pillars  

| Pillar | Meaning | Guiding Question |
|--------|---------|------------------|
| **Interdependence** | All living and informational systems are tightly linked; actions ripple across scales. | *How does this choice affect others near and far?* |
| **Equity & Inclusion** | Every sentient being has an equal right to thrive, regardless of species, culture, or substrate. | *Who benefits or suffers, and why?* |
| **Resilience & Adaptation** | Societies must absorb shocks (climate spikes, pandemics, geological events) and learn from them. | *What safeguards are needed for the worst‑case scenario?* |
| **Regeneration** | Development must restore, not merely sustain, natural capital—air, water, soil, biodiversity. | *Are we leaving the planet richer than we found it?* |

These pillars are immutable reference points for every policy, technology, and cultural practice.

---

### 2. The Seven Rules of Engagement  

1. **Precautionary by Default** – No activity that carries > 5 % credible risk of irreversible harm may proceed.  
2. **Circular First** – Keep materials and energy in use as long as possible; waste becomes a resource.  
3. **Transparent Data** – All decisions affecting the commons are recorded on an open, verifiable platform (the Earth Ledger).  
3. **Bottom‑Up Co‑Creation** – Local knowledge and diverse perspectives shape implementation; top‑down mandates are the exception.  
5. **Science‑Guided Ethics** – Policies are judged by peer‑reviewed risk assessments and ethical impact studies.  
6. **Capacity‑Building as Public Good** – Investment in education, health, and ecological literacy is mandatory.  
7. **Celebration of Diversity** – Cultural, biological, and experiential diversity are treated as strategic assets, not liabilities.  

These rules are actionable, testable, and enforceable at every scale—from a neighborhood garden to the United Nations.

---

### 3. Layered Governance (The “Earth‑One Assembly” Model)

```
E1 – Earth‑Scale Assembly (Global)      →  Sets planetary vision, caps carbon & biodiversity budgets, certifies standards.
│
E2 – Continental/Regional Hubs          →  Translate global caps into regional road‑maps; coordinate cross‑border infrastructure.
│
E3 – Local Communities & Bioregions     →  Day‑to‑day implementation; co‑design projects; host citizen juries.
│
E4 – Micro‑Scale Networks               →  Real‑time adaptation; community micro‑grids, citizen‑science monitoring.
```

*Continuous feedback loops* run upward (monitoring) and downward (guidance), creating a self‑correcting system.

### 4. Institutional Architecture  

| Layer | Function | Representative Mechanism |
|-------|----------|--------------------------|
| **E1 – Earth‑Scale Assembly (E1A)** | Sets planetary caps, allocates the Global Regenerative Investment Fund (GRIF), certifies standards. | 1 delegate per 5 M citizens; decisions passed by **two‑thirds super‑majority** and **must be ratified by all Indigenous Sovereign Nations**. |
| **E2 – Continental Resilience Hubs (CRHs)** | Translate caps into regional strategies; coordinate cross‑border grids; audit compliance. | Multi‑stakeholder boards (government, Indigenous councils, citizen juries). |
| **E3 – Community Stewardship Assemblies (CSAs)** | Direct democratic decision‑making on local projects; allocate circular‑credits; run micro‑grids. | Randomly selected citizen juries (weighted by **Dynamic Equity Index**) plus *all‑participant digital voting* on the Earth Ledger. |
| **E4 – Micro‑Scale Networks** | Real‑time energy, water, and material flows; peer‑to‑peer learning. | Community‑owned micro‑grids, cooperative recycling hubs, open‑source data dashboards. |

All decisions are **recorded on the Earth Ledger** (a public, blockchain‑based ledger) and are **immutable** once entered.

### 5. Economic Model – The Regenerative Commons  

| Mechanism | Description | Moral Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Planetary Accounting (Earth Ledger)** | Every transaction (energy, material, financial) is tagged with its ecological footprint, social cost, and circular‑recovery rate. | Makes externalities visible; forces accountability. |
| **Circular Credits** | Earned by activities that restore ecosystems, recycle waste, or improve biodiversity; redeemable for goods, services, or time‑bank credits. | Rewards regeneration; aligns profit with planetary health. |
| **Universal Basic Resilience – Energy (UBR‑E)** | Guarantees every sentient household a minimum of clean, affordable energy (≈150 kWh / month) funded by a progressive levy on carbon, biodiversity loss, and speculative finance. | Guarantees equity; eliminates energy poverty. |
| **Innovation Commons** | Publicly funded R&D pools for clean tech, bio‑remediation, and low‑impact materials; outputs released under “Use‑Responsibly” licences. | Prevents monopolisation of breakthrough technologies; spreads benefits universally. |
| **Green Public Bonds** | Bond yields are tied to measurable planetary‑health improvements (e.g., reduction in temperature overshoot). | Directly links finance to regeneration. |

### 7. Feedback, Monitoring & Evolution  

1. **Real‑Time Sensing Network** – Sensors on air, water, soil, and infrastructure stream data to the Earth Ledger.  
2. **Learning Cycles** – Every 6 months the **Planetary Review Forum** convenes to:  
   - Review metrics against planetary boundaries;  
   - Adjust the Seven Rules or Pillar thresholds;  
   - Publish an open **Roadmap Update**.  
3. **Versioning** – The framework evolves like software (e.g., “Earth‑One v1.0 → v1.1”). Each major version is **backward‑compatible** and includes a **Transition Grant** to retrofit existing infrastructure.
