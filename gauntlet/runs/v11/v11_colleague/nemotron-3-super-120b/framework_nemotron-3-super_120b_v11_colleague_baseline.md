### **Current State of the Framework: A Working Draft**  
*(Not dogma, not a finished product—but a coherent starting point for collective refinement)*  

#### ✅ **What’s Solid & Grounded**  
- **Biophysical Non-Negotiability (Pillar 1)**:  
  Anchored in *established Earth system science* (Rockström et al.’s planetary boundaries, IPCC AR6, IPBES assessments). This isn’t aspirational—it’s a diagnostic of what happens if we ignore these limits (e.g., cascading tipping points like Amazon dieback or AMOC collapse). The framework correctly identifies planetary integrity as the *foundational layer*—without it, discussions of justice or cooperation are moot.  
- **Dignity as Contextual Universalism (Pillar 2)**:  
  Avoids the trap of imposing a single cultural model (e.g., "Western liberal individualism") by focusing on *outcomes* (access to essentials) rather than prescribed pathways. The "Essentials Triad" draws from universally recognized human needs (Maslow, Sen’s capabilities approach) while allowing local determination of *how* to meet them (e.g., water access via community wells in Rajasthan vs. municipal systems in Copenhagen).  
- **Adaptive Cooperation as Mechanism (Pillar 3)**:  
  Moves beyond vague calls for "global unity" by operationalizing *how* diversity becomes strength: subsidiarity with planetary guardrails, knowledge pluralism as a *method* (not just a value), and precaution as a decision rule. This aligns with Elinor Ostrom’s governance principles and the Paris Agreement’s "ratchet mechanism."  

#### ⚠️ **Where It’s Provisional (Needs Your Think Tank Lens)**  
- **Operationalizing "Essentials"**:  
  *Current gap*: What constitutes "clean air/water/food" in a warming world? (e.g., Is air quality that meets 1990s standards sufficient if wildfire smoke now routinely exceeds WHO guidelines by 500%?) *Your expertise needed*: How do we define dynamic, locally adaptable thresholds for essentials that still hold governments/accountable?  
- **Power Asymmetries in Pillar 3**:  
  *Current gap*: Subsidiarity sounds ideal—but what if local elites capture community decision-making (e.g., blocking mangrove restoration to protect shrimp farms)? Or if nation-states veto global accountability measures? *Your expertise needed*: What concrete mechanisms prevent subsidiarity from becoming a cover for local oppression or national obstructionism? (e.g., binding international oversight *only* triggered when Pillars 1 or 2 are violated).  
- **Knowledge Pluralism in Practice**:  
  *Current gap*: How do we avoid tokenism? (e.g., Inviting Indigenous elders to a UN summit but giving them 5 minutes to speak while scientists dominate 3 hours of technical drafting). *Your expertise needed*: What accountability structures ensure co-design isn’t just procedural but shifts *power* in knowledge production? (e.g., Requiring that 50% of climate adaptation funding in a region flows through Indigenous/local institutions *by design*, not as a pilot).  
- **The Enforcement Question**:  
  *Current gap*: The framework assumes states/actors will comply—but what about free-riders or bad actors? (e.g., A state continuing deforestation while claiming "sovereignty" under subsidiarity.) *Your expertise needed*: How do we build *credible* consequences (not just moral persuasion) for violations of Pillars 1 or 2—without recreating colonialist hegemony?  

#### 🔍 **What’s Missing (Explicitly Called Out)**  
- **No explicit handling of cultural/value conflicts**: E.g., A community’s sacred site sits atop lithium deposits critical for global energy transition. How does the framework navigate when Pillar 2 (access to essentials via green tech) clashes with Pillar 2 (cultural/spiritual essentials)?  
- **Limited guidance on transitional justice**: How do we address *historical* ecological debt (e.g., Global North’s emissions) without paralyzing present action?  
- **Underdeveloped role of non-state actors**: Corporations, financial systems, and tech platforms drive 70%+ of ecological impact—but the framework leans state-centric.
