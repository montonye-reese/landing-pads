### **I. The Three Non-Negotiable Pillars**  
*(Each pillar includes: Principle → Foundation → Implication)*  

1. **Planetary Interdependence**  
   - *Principle:* Earth is a single, finite biophysical system. Human actions alter atmospheric composition, ocean chemistry, biodiversity, and climate globally—no nation or community is ecologically isolated.  
   - *Foundation:* Scientific consensus (e.g., IPCC, planetary boundaries framework) shows that exceeding thresholds (like 1.5°C warming, biodiversity loss, or nitrogen pollution) destabilizes the conditions for agriculture, health, and stability *everywhere*.  
   - *Implication:* All societies must operate within Earth’s regenerative capacity. This isn’t "environmentalism"—it’s basic physics and biology. Violating it harms *all* inhabitants, though inequitably.  

2. **Inherent Human Dignity**  
   - *Principle:* Every person possesses intrinsic worth simply by being human—regardless of citizenship, identity, ability, or status. This is not culturally relative; it arises from our shared capacity for suffering, joy, aspiration, and mutual recognition.  
   - *Foundation:* Cross-cultural ethical traditions (Ubuntu, *Ahimsa*, Kantian dignity, Indigenous concepts of relational personhood) converge on the rejection of torture, slavery, dehumanization, and deny that any life is "less valuable." Modern human rights law codifies this as a floor, not a ceiling.  
   - *Implication:* No system can justify exploiting, discarding, or inflicting severe harm on people as "acceptable collateral." Dignity requires access to *minimum conditions* for agency: freedom from violence, basic sustenance, and the ability to participate in decisions affecting one’s life.  

3. **Reciprocal Responsibility**  
   - *Principle:* Our survival and dignity depend on mutual aid and stewardship. We are born dependent (on caregivers, ecosystems, knowledge) and remain interdependent throughout life.  
   - *Foundation:* Anthropology shows all human societies rely on reciprocity (gift economies, kinship, communal labor). Ecology confirms that species (including humans) thrive via symbiosis—not pure competition. Ignoring this fractures social cohesion and ecological resilience.  
   - *Implication:* Rights imply responsibilities: to not harm others’ dignity, to contribute to communal well-being per one’s capacity, and to safeguard the shared Earth for future generations.

### **II. The Four Guiding Principles**  
*(How the pillars translate into action)*  

1. **Precautionary Stewardship**  
   - When actions risk irreversible harm to planetary systems or human dignity (e.g., releasing novel toxins, eroding democratic norms), the burden of proof lies on those proposing the action—not on proving harm *after* it occurs.  

2. **Proportional Justice**  
   - Burdens and benefits of cooperation (e.g., climate action, pandemic response) must be shared fairly, prioritizing those most vulnerable or historically marginalized—but *without* exempting anyone from the pillars.  

3. **Pluralistic Autonomy**  
   - Communities self-determine *how* to uphold the pillars (e.g., governance, spirituality, economics) as long as they don’t violate others’ dignity or planetary boundaries. This rejects both tyranny of the majority and cultural relativism that excuses harm.  

4. **Adaptive Learning**  
   - Frameworks must evolve with new knowledge (science, lived experience) and changing conditions. Rigidity breeds failure; dogmatism fuels conflict.

### **III. What the Framework Explicitly Is Not**  
*(Directly stated boundaries)*  

- ❌ **Not a world government:** It sets *conditions* for legitimacy (e.g., "A government violating dignity or destroying its ecological base forfeits consent"), but leaves sovereignty and governance models open.

- ❌ **Not utopian:** It accepts conflict and imperfection but insists that systems must be *judged* by whether they reduce unnecessary suffering, expand dignity’s preconditions, and stay within planetary bounds.
