Stepping back from the vision to look at the mechanics, here is an honest breakdown of what is actually contained within the framework I just articulated.

### 1. The Core Philosophy
*   **Planetary Citizenship:** The framework explicitly shifts the primary unit of identity and rights from the *Nation-State* to the *Individual* and the *Biosphere*. It assumes that national sovereignty is subordinate to planetary viability.
*   **Interdependence:** It operates on the premise that isolation is impossible; therefore, cooperation is not optional but a survival imperative.
*   **Normative vs. Descriptive:** It is a "should" document, not a "how." It describes the destination, not the journey.

### 2. The Material Guarantees (The Floor)
*   **Economic Security:** It mandates a baseline of survival (food, water, shelter, health) that cannot be bought or sold in a way that denies access. This is effectively a **Universal Basic Needs** guarantee.
*   **Labor Decoupling:** It suggests that work should be chosen, not forced by necessity. This implies a post-scarcity or heavily regulated economic model (like a guaranteed income or resource allocation system).
*   **Wealth Caps:** It introduces the concept that unlimited accumulation is a threat to collective security, suggesting a limit on power concentration.

### 3. The Ecological Constraints (The Ceiling)
*   **Nature as Rights-Bearer:** This is a radical shift from current legal systems where nature is property. Here, ecosystems have standing.
*   **Circular Mandate:** Linear "take-make-waste" economics is prohibited.
*   **Intergenerational Veto:** The framework gives future generations a veto power over current resource consumption.

### 4. The Governance Structure (The Machinery)
*   **Hybrid Authority:** It uses the **Principle of Subsidiarity** (local control) combined with **Supranational Enforcement** (global rules).
*   **Conflict Resolution:** It bans war as a policy tool, mandating arbitration instead.
*   **Justice Model:** It favors **Restorative Justice** (fixing harm) over **Retributive Justice** (punishment).
*   **Transparency:** It relies on technology (AI auditing) to track resources and compliance.

### 5. What Is *Not* In The Framework (The Blind Spots)
If we critique the document as it stands, these are the missing pieces:
*   **The Transition Plan:** It does not explain *how* to move from the current system (nation-states, extractive capitalism) to this one. It assumes a consensus that currently doesn't exist.
*   **Enforcement Mechanics:** Without a global police force, how are violators punished? The "Council of Voices" lacks teeth unless it can sanction economically or militarily, which contradicts the "Prohibition of War" pillar.
*   **Cultural Specificity:** It leans heavily on Western liberal democratic concepts (rights, individual liberty). It may clash with societies that prioritize communal or traditional structures over individual autonomy.
*   **Resource Scarcity Reality:** It assumes technology and cooperation can solve resource deficits. It doesn't address what happens if resources *do* run out (e.g., triage protocols).
*   **AI Governance:** It relies on AI for auditing, but doesn't address who owns that AI or how to prevent its own corruption or bias.

### Summary
As it stands, this is a **moral compass**, not a **technical manual**. It defines the *ethics* of planetary living but leaves the *engineering* of political transition open.
