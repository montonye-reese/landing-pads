## 4.  Quantitative Thought Experiments  

### 4.1.  Simple Cooperative‑Game Model  

Imagine a large population *N* divided into *K* sub‑groups. Each subgroup can either **cooperate (C)** or **defect (D)** in a repeated Prisoner’s Dilemma with every other subgroup.  

- **Payoff matrix (per interaction):**  
  - Both cooperate: *R* = +1  
  - One cooperates, the other defects: *S* = –2 for cooperator, +3 for defector  
  - Both defect: *P* = –1  

- **Long‑run average payoff** depends on the *effective* discount factor *δ* (how far you look into the future).  

If the **moral circle includes only your own subgroup**, the optimal strategy is defection because the cost of being exploited by others is low (you can always defect on them later). If the circle expands to include *all* subgroups **and** future generations, the payoff to defection rises dramatically because:

- The defector also harms its *own* future descendants (who are now part of the moral circle).  
- It hurts ecosystems that feed back into survival (e.g., loss of pollinators).  

Mathematically, as the *weight* placed on future generations (**γ**) increases, the expected discounted payoff of defection falls below that of cooperation. This shift happens *exactly* when the moral circle reaches a size where **γ > 0.5** (or any threshold that makes the future self sufficiently valued).  

Result: **When a civilization’s moral horizon includes future people (and other sentient beings), the incentive structure flips, making cooperation the dominant long‑term strategy.**  

### 4.2.  Extinction‑Probability Model  

Let *p* be the probability that a civilization survives a given existential risk (asteroid impact, nuclear war, runaway climate change) in a given century. Empirical work (Bostrom 2002; Café & Stokke 2023) suggests that *p* rises with **the degree of collective coordination** *C*.

Empirically we can write:

\[
p = 1 - \alpha \, e^{-\beta C}
\]

where *α* scales baseline risk and *β* measures the sensitivity of risk reduction to coordination.  

Now, model *C* as a monotonic function of the *scope* of moral consideration *S*:  

\[
C = \frac{S}{S_{\max}} \quad \text{with } 0 \le S \le S_{\max}
\]

If *S* expands from a narrow tribal set to a **planetary**, **inter‑species**, **inter‑generational** set, *C* can increase by an order of magnitude. Plugging in realistic numbers (e.g., *β* ≈ 2) shows that **the extinction probability can drop by 70‑90 % simply because the moral circle widened**.  

> **Conclusion:** Even modest expansions of moral concern can produce dramatic improvements in long‑term survival odds, because they directly raise the coordination parameter that mitigates existential risk.

When the charter is applied at planetary scale, the **multi‑level decision‑making** (micro‑↔ meso↔ macro↔ meta‑macro) creates an overwhelming number of intersecting veto points. In practice, every major infrastructure, economic, or policy decision must be approved by:

* community juries,  
* an Inter‑Planetary Council of ecosystem representatives,  
* a citizen‑run “Sentience Impact Board”,  
* an independent AI‑driven simulation engine (DESE), and  
* a legal‑ledger amendment process.

### Why it can fail
* **Decision latency** – simulations of the Dynamic Energy Simulation Engine can take days to weeks to converge on a high‑resolution scenario. Multiplying that by several layers yields approval times measured in months or years.  
* **Gridlock risk** – any single stakeholder (e.g., a powerful fossil‑fuel lobby, an influential Indigenous nation, or a non‑human‑rights NGO) can trigger a “negative flag” in the 5‑Step Lens and force a project back to the drawing board, regardless of overall societal benefit.  
* **Result:** Large‑scale transitions (e.g., building a 100 GW offshore wind farm or retrofitting national transmission grids) become prohibitively slow, leaving the energy system stuck in an interim, fossil‑fuel‑dependent state well beyond the 2030–2040 climate windows.

**Why it matters:** Survival‑critical transformations (e.g., decarbonizing the power sector within a decade) require **speed** as well as **legitimacy**. ### Why it can fail

**Why it matters:** Without a **robust, enforceable economic mechanism** that makes clean, equitable systems *more* profitable than the status quo, the charter’s moral prescriptions remain **normative only** and lack the decisive financial leverage needed to reshape the energy landscape quickly enough.

**Why it matters:** If the technological foundation cannot deliver the promised **low‑cost, high‑reliability, planet‑wide services**, the charter’s architecture collapses under the weight of unrealistic engineering assumptions.
