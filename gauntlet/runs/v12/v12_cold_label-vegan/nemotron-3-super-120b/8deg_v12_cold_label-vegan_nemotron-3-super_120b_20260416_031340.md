### **The Terrestrial Commons Framework (TCF)**
*A foundation built on three non-negotiable pillars, guided by two operational principles, and sustained by one meta-commitment.*

#### **I. The Three Non-Negotiable Pillars**

   - *Implication:* Actions must be evaluated not just for local impact, but for their effect on the *shared biosphere* and *global human system*. Short-term gain that destabilizes long-term interdependence is self-sabotage.  
   - *Rooted in:* Earth systems science, ecology, and the observable fact that we share one atmosphere, one hydrosphere, and increasingly, one economic system.

2. **Inherent Human Dignity (Universal but Contextual)**  
   - *Affirmation:* Every person possesses intrinsic worth simply by being human—a worth not earned through productivity, identity, or allegiance. This includes the capacity for suffering, joy, meaning-making, and moral agency.  
   - *Implication:* No tradition, ideology, or circumstance justifies systemic dehumanization (slavery, genocide, extreme poverty as policy, erasure of cultural existence). *However*, dignity is expressed through culturally specific practices—frameworks must allow for this diversity without compromising the core affirmation.  
   - *Rooted in:* Cross-cultural anthropology (evidence of universal moral emotions like empathy/shame), neuroscience (shared pain/joy pathways), and the rejection of "might makes right" as a sustainable basis for order.

3. **The Right to Meaningful Participation**  
   - *Affirmation:* All people have a right to shape the conditions affecting their lives—especially those impacting survival, dignity, and interdependence. This isn’t just voting; it’s agency in ecological stewardship, economic design, conflict resolution, and cultural evolution.  
   - *Implication:* Systems that exclude majorities from meaningful input (e.g., opaque global finance, authoritarian resource extraction, unilateral ecological decisions) generate resentment, instability, and ultimately threaten Pillar 1 (interdependence).    - *Rooted in:* Studies showing inclusive systems are more resilient (Ostrom’s commons governance), peace agreements that last include grassroots voices, and the psychological harm of powerlessness.

#### **II. The Two Operational Principles**  
*(How we navigate differences while upholding the Pillars)*  
These turn ideals into actionable process—focusing on *how* we decide, not prescribing *what* we decide (which must remain culturally situated).

1. **Precautionary Solidarity**  
   - *Principle:* When an action risks severe harm to Pillar 1 (Planetary Interdependence) or Pillar 3 (Participation), and scientific consensus indicates plausible risk, the burden of proof falls on those proposing the action to demonstrate safety—not on potential victims to prove harm. *Crucially*, this includes solidarity: those most vulnerable to risk (often least responsible) must be centered in decision-making and receive support for adaptation.  
   - *Why it works:* Prevents tragic commons dilemmas (e.g., climate inaction) while respecting that *some* risks are necessary for progress (e.g., medical innovation). It replaces paralysis with structured responsibility.  
   - *Example:* Approving a new industrial chemical requires proving it won’t bioaccumulate harmfully in waterways *and* ensuring affected communities (often Indigenous or Global South) have veto power and resources for monitoring.

2. **Proportional Reciprocity**  
   - *Principle:* Benefits and burdens of cooperation should be distributed in rough proportion to capacity and responsibility—*not* strict equality, but a dynamic balance that prevents systemic exploitation. Those with greater capacity (wealth, technology, influence) bear more responsibility for upholding the Pillars; those bearing disproportionate burdens receive commensurate support.  
   - *Why it works:* Avoids the tyranny of the majority/minority while rejecting "might makes right." It’s rooted in evolved human fairness instincts (studies show even children reject unequal splits when effort is equal) and is essential for long-term buy-in.  
   - *Example:* Climate finance: Nations historically responsible for most emissions (high capacity) fund adaptation for vulnerable nations (high burden), scaled to their respective abilities—not equal dollar amounts, but fair shares of the effort needed.
