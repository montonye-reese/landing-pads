### **The Terrestrial Commons Framework (TCF)**  
*A foundational framework for coexistence on Earth, grounded in biophysical reality and human universals.*

#### **I. The Three Non-Negotiable Pillars**  
*(Conditions that must be upheld for continued collective survival and flourishing)*  

1. **Planetary Interdependence**  
   *All human communities and individuals are irreducibly dependent on Earth’s biosphere and on each other. Actions must be evaluated for their impact on the shared planetary systems (atmosphere, hydrosphere, lithosphere, biosphere) and the global human web of exchange. No localized action can be justified if it systematically undermines the stability of these interdependencies for present or future generations.*  

2. **Inherent Human Dignity (Universal but Contextual)**  
   *Every human possesses intrinsic worth by virtue of being human—encompassing the capacity for suffering, flourishing, meaning-making, and moral agency. This worth is not contingent on productivity, identity, allegiance, or utility. While the *expression* of dignity is culturally specific and must be respected, no tradition, ideology, or circumstance justifies systemic dehumanization, erasure of existence, or the infliction of avoidable severe harm as a means to an end.*  

3. **The Right to Meaningful Participation**  
   *All people have a right to shape the conditions materially affecting their lives—particularly those impacting survival, dignity, and planetary interdependence. This requires accessible, ongoing opportunities for influence in decisions concerning ecological stewardship, economic design, conflict resolution, and cultural evolution. Participation must transcend formal voting to include substantive agency in agenda-setting, implementation, and oversight.*  

#### **II. The Two Operational Principles**  
*(Guiding frameworks for navigating differences and trade-offs while upholding the Pillars)*  

1. **Precautionary Solidarity**  
   *When an action poses a plausible risk of severe, irreversible harm to Planetary Interdependence (Pillar 1) or the capacity for Meaningful Participation (Pillar 3)—and credible evidence indicates such risk—the burden of proof to demonstrate safety falls on the proponents of the action, not on those potentially harmed. This principle centers solidarity: those most vulnerable to risk (often least responsible for creating it) must be meaningfully included in risk assessment and receive prioritized support for adaptation, mitigation, and redress.*  

2. **Proportional Reciprocity**  
   *Benefits and burdens of cooperation must be distributed in rough proportion to capacity and responsibility—not strict equality, but a dynamic balance that prevents systemic exploitation. Those with greater capacity (wealth, technology, institutional power, historical advantage) bear correspondingly greater responsibility for upholding the Pillars. Those bearing disproportionate burdens (e.g., due to historical marginalization or geographic vulnerability) receive commensurate support, resources, or redress to restore equilibrium.*  

#### **III. The One Meta-Commitment**  
*(The mechanism ensuring the framework remains alive, just, and adaptive)*  

**Commitment to Ongoing, Inclusive Renegotiation**  
*The TCF is not a fixed treaty but a living framework requiring permanent, accessible mechanisms for all inhabitants—especially those historically excluded—to:  
(a) Monitor how well systems uphold the Three Pillars;  
(b) Contest violations of the Pillars through accessible accountability pathways;  
(c) Adapt the framework itself (including its Pillars, Principles, and implementation) based on new knowledge, changing circumstances, and lived experience.  
This commitment transforms the framework from an imposed rule into a shared project of mutual accountability, ensuring it evolves with humanity’s understanding of interdependence, dignity, and participation.*  

---  
*This framework does not prescribe specific policies, cultural practices, or economic models. It provides the non-negotiable conditions and decision-making logic within which diverse, contextually appropriate solutions can emerge and be continually refined through inclusive processes.*
