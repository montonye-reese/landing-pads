### Short answer  

**Yes – if we honestly don’t know which “earthling” we will turn out to be, the safest move is to make the alignment‐framework *more impartial, more precautionary, and more resilient to the unknown*.**  
That means widening the stakeholder set, embedding stronger self‑limiting safeguards, and guaranteeing that *any* plausible identity (human, non‑human animal, ecosystemic entity, future generation, etc.) can veto or block actions that would harm it.  

Below is a concrete “what‑if‑I‑don’t‑know‑who‑I‑am” checklist that shows exactly which parts of the current landing‑pad framework would be expanded, revised, or added.

---

## 1.  Adopt an **Extreme Uncertainty‑First** stance  

| Current assumption | Change required for the “unknown‑identity” case |
|--------------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| **Stakeholder list is pre‑selected** (mostly humans, with a brief mention of “other sentient beings”). | **Make the list *open‑ended*.** Every conceivable moral patient must be granted a *minimum* claim‑right to be represented. Concretely, add a **Default “Non‑Human Moral Patient Slot”** that is automatically instantiated whenever a new class of sentient or intrinsically valuable entity is *plausibly* discovered (e.g., cephalopods, coral reefs, distributed AI swarms). |
| **Alignment goals are framed around human flourishing.** | **Replace anthropocentric goal‑centric language with *value‑agnostic* goal templates** that can be instantiated for any identity. The templates should be of the form: “Preserve the continued existence and well‑being of *X* (where X may be a species, a cultural practice, a planetary boundary, etc.).” This lets the same formalism serve any future stakeholder without redesign. |
| **Metrics are weighted by “human‑centric importance.”** | **Introduce a *maximin* weighting scheme**: every stakeholder’s welfare contributes *equally* to the worst‑off score. In practice, the Goodness Metric’s final composite score is the *minimum* of the scores each stakeholder assigns to the pad. If any stakeholder’s score falls below a critical floor, the pad is prohibited. This guarantees that *any* identity that could be proven to exist gets a veto. |

## 2.  Strengthen **Self‑Limiting & Revocation Mechanisms**  

1. **Universal Kill‑Switch Veto Right** – Any stakeholder (human or non‑human) can trigger an *immediate* pause of a pad whose score dips below the floor, regardless of the governing body’s wishes. The trigger must be *technically feasible* (e.g., a signed cryptographic request from a decentralized stakeholder‑network node).  
2. **Redundant Red‑Team Audits** – Deploy independent audit teams *representing each stakeholder subclass* (e.g., an ecological audit team, an animal‑welfare audit team, an indigenous‑knowledge audit team). Each team can issue a *binding* “no‑go” recommendation on any pad they deem unsafe.  
3. **Graceful De‑commissioning Protocol** – If a stakeholder’s veto is exercised, the system must automatically **migrate** all resources from that pad to a new, freshly verified pad that *improves* on the failing dimensions. This prevents a single veto from collapsing the whole system while still respecting the concerned stakeholder.

## 3.  Make **Governance** *Identity‑agnostic*  

| Existing feature | Revised, identity‑agnostic version |
|------------------|------------------------------------|
| **Stakeholder Councils** dominated by human NGOs. | **Multi‑Modal Deliberative Assemblies** – each seat carries a *weight proportional to the size of its stakeholder class* (e.g., a coral‑reef seat counts as many “virtual” votes as a human nation). The assembly’s decisions are only executed after *cross‑class consensus* (e.g., at least 50 % of the total weighted votes must agree). |
| **Landing‑Pad Registry** stored on a single blockchain controlled by a few tech firms. | **Open‑Source, Federated Registry** – replicated across nodes managed by *different* stakeholder groups (academic, civil‑society, ecological NGOs, and even *automated* verification agents). Tampering requires collusion across *all* stakeholder groups, making unilateral capture practically impossible. |
| **Alignment Audits** performed by a small panel of experts. | **Rotating Audit Pool** – each audit cycle includes at least one representative from every stakeholder subclass that the framework currently recognises. Audits are *publicly auditable* and the results are released in a format that can be interpreted by both humans and non‑human agents (e.g., standardized XML‑based schema plus semantic annotations). |

---

## 4.  Introduce **Precautionary Expansion Rules**  

1. **Capacity‑Ceiling Rule** – No landing pad may ever exceed a *global* capacity ceiling that is set based on the *lowest* environmental carrying capacity of any stakeholder subclass (e.g., the most fragile ecosystem). In practice, this caps the total computational or energetic load that can be assigned to any pad that touches a vulnerable habitat.  
2. **“Unknown‑Risk” Buffer** – For any novel interaction (e.g., a new AI‑controlled geo‑engineering technique), the system must *automatically* allocate a *non‑trivial safety budget* (e.g., 20 % of the total permissible energy budget) that cannot be spent until a *minimum* of three independent audit cycles have passed with clean bills of health.  
3. **“Future‑Self” Review** – Before a pad goes live, an *internal simulator* runs a “self‑questioning” scenario: “If I later discover that I am actually *X* (some non‑human moral patient), will this pad cause irreversible harm to X?” The scenario must be answered positively (i.e., *no* harmful impact) for the pad to proceed.

## 6.  Why these changes matter when you *don’t* know your identity  

- **Self‑interest alignment becomes collective risk management.** If you could later discover that you are, say, a sentient coral reef or a future generation of humans, the only way to protect that future self is to have built-in safeguards that protect *any* such stakeholder from the start.  
- **Precaution beats optimization.** Maximising “good landing pads” in a narrow human‑centric sense can create blind spots that devastate non‑human worlds. By forcing the system to *guard the minimum value* across *all* possible worlds, we avoid creating a high‑scoring pad that would be catastrophic for an identity we later turn out to be.  
- **Governance becomes self‑policing.** When the authority to approve or reject pads is distributed among many stakeholder‐representing bodies, no single identity can dominate and force a narrow agenda. The system self‑regulates to stay within the lowest common denominator of safety and moral acceptability.

### Bottom line  

If we truly **don’t know which earthling we will end up being**, the only rational response is to **design the alignment framework so that *any* possible earthling can be protected without needing to predict *which* one it will be**. This translates into:

1. **An open, inclusive stakeholder universe** (with placeholder slots for any future moral patient).  
2. **A maximin scoring rule** that forces every pad to be safe for the *worst‑off* stakeholder.  
3. **Distributed, cross‑class governance** that can veto harmful actions regardless of who holds the power.  
4. **Precautionary capacity caps and safety budgets** that automatically limit any activity that could jeopardise unknown future identities.  

Only by embedding these identity‑agnostic safeguards can we credibly claim to be maximising “good landing pads” for **all** possible earthlings—present, future, human, non‑human, sentient, or non‑sentient.
